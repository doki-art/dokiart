Case 02 / Martech · B2B SaaS

Eliminating a support queue with one design decision

Salesflow needed to be resold under partner brands — added after the product had shipped, with limited resources and no theming infrastructure. The visible result is a logo, a name, and a few colour controls any user can set themselves. The work was finding out how little needed to change.

The work

The problem

Salesflow had already shipped when the business needed it to be resellable under partner brands — partly to take load off support, partly because self-serve branding was a gap in what sales could offer. It was a revenue and positioning need, not a cosmetic one.

The obvious way to deliver it was a full theming layer: a large build, and a real risk that partners would produce broken-looking interfaces and end up contacting support anyway. The expensive solution might not even solve the problem it was meant for.

The insight

So before designing anything, I talked to support — what do partners actually ask for? The answer reframed the scope. Users didn't want to theme a product; they wanted it to feel like theirs.

And that feeling rested on very little: the logo, the name, the primary actions, a few accents. Everything else was structure they never noticed and never asked to change. The huge, complex problem in the original brief was mostly imaginary.

The system

I reviewed the whole system and redesigned the few elements that would carry a partner's brand, so they could take any colour cleanly without affecting legibility or state. For partners with no designer, the system generates a coherent palette automatically — the default path produces something that looks intentional, not improvised.

The result is a control surface any user can configure on their own. It was later rebuilt into the design system as a white-label mode running alongside the standard one, on a shared structural foundation.

/ 03
Outcome

What shipped, and what stopped happening

White-label went live as something sales could offer, at a fraction of the cost of a theming engine.

—

Support tickets dropped to a minimum

The branding questions the work was meant to absorb largely stopped arriving. Partners can't misconfigure what was never left configurable.

—

Built to last

The system is transparent and easy to maintain — it's held up for years without becoming a source of edge cases.

—

A reusable decision

"Find out how little needs to change before building" became a default move on later system work.

/ 04
Reflection

The instinct under a constraint like this is to scope the proper solution and ask for time to build it. The more useful move was to check whether the proper solution was even needed — and the answer came from a conversation with support, not from the design tool.

It's the least visually loud thing I've shipped and one of the decisions I'd most want to be asked about, because the value is in how little ended up on the screen.
